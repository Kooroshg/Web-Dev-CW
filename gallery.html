Education is not only a fundamental human right but also a key investment in human capital that translates into economic growth and other development outcomes. Keeping children in lower-income countries in school longer is one of the best global investments that can be made. Returns to education, particularly at higher education levels, are highest in low-income countries.
“We are failing children. Too many education systems around the world are investing the least in those children who need it the most,” said UNICEF Executive Director Catherine Russell. “Investing in the education of the poorest children is the most cost-effective way to ensure the future for children, communities and countries. True progress can only come when we invest in every child, everywhere.”
Despite the numerous benefits of education, many low-income countries struggle to provide adequate educational opportunities. According to data from the UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS), an estimated 258 million children and youth worldwide were out of school in 2018. The majority of these out-of-school children live in low- and lower-middle-income countries, with Sub-Saharan Africa being the region with the highest out-of-school rates. The education system in low-income countries faces various challenges, for instance lack of resources and infrastructure, low enrollment rates, and insufficient teacher training and salaries.

This is true for all children, but particularly true for disadvantaged students from low-income contexts. This combination of poor preparation and low motivation is lethal in terms of children learning.
